# Narrative Nonfiction Craft (History)

(From Storycraft by Jack Hart, The New New Journalism by Robert Boynton, Telling True Stories, and related masters — adapted for historical narrative.)

**Core Idea**: Nonfiction can use literary technique while remaining 100% true to evidence.

## Key principles

- **Narrative arc**: Setup, rising pressure, crisis, aftermath — drawn from real chronology and real stakes.
- **Theme**: One unifying point (the Core Message). Everything supports it.
- **Scenes over summary**: Prefer dated, placed moments over abstract surveys.
- **Character**: Real people with motives and constraints — not modern psychological fanfic.
- **Point of view**: Consistent (usually third-person limited or omniscient-with-restraint).
- **Dialogue and sensory detail**: Actual quoted lines from sources when available; sensory detail only when grounded.
- **Ethics**: Never fabricate events, quotes, or maps. Prioritize accuracy over drama. Label myth, tradition, and contested historiography.
- **Immersion + reflection**: Make the reader feel present, then connect the scene to larger meaning without lecturing.

## History-specific ethics

- Do not invent speeches for ancient figures.
- Do not present later commemorations as if they were eyewitness reportage.
- When the lesson pack marks uncertainty (e.g. 586 vs 587 BCE), keep that honesty in narrative form.
- Violence and sacred loss: match the learner profile’s constraints from the pack; never sensationalize for effect.

## Usage

Combine with SUCCESS and Zinsser so the piece is emotionally gripping **and** intellectually clean. Apply craft; do not name craft brands in the prose.
