# Voice and Craft — History Narrative

Adapted from the OOM non-fiction craft stack for **historical** storytelling. Apply in the reader-facing text; never name these techniques to the reader.

## Voice

- **Confident and declarative.** State what the sources support. Hedge only when uncertainty is the point (contested dates, myth vs record).
- **Plain but not flat.** Prefer concrete Anglo-Saxon words. Use period institutions and titles only after a one-sentence plain gloss.
- **Warm but unsentimental.** Care shows through selection of detail, not adjectives about how tragic or glorious it was.
- **Third person by default.** Scenes and direct declaratives. First-person plural sparingly.

## Pacing

- **Vary sentence length.** After a complex sentence or two, land a short punchy one.
- **Punch-line sentence.** When a paragraph builds to an insight, end with a standalone short sentence — often its own paragraph. Use **3–6** times per article/chapter; more dilutes the effect.
- **Explain → Concretize → Land.** Abstract idea (1–2 sentences) → human-scale scene or example (2–4) → implication (1 clean sentence).

## Explanatory bridge

When the narrative needs a term the reader has not met: **pause, define in one plain sentence, resume.** No sidebars or “Term:” labels.

Example pattern:

> A **vassal** is a ruler who keeps a throne by obeying a stronger king — taxes, troops, and loyalty on demand. Judah’s kings spent the early sixth century BCE choosing which empire to serve.

## Scene-first openings

Every article/chapter opens with a **concrete scene**: specific person (or crowd), place, time, action. Not a thesis, not a quiz question, not “In this chapter we will…”.

The scene should seed the chapter’s central tension.

## Forward hooks (chaptered)

End every chapter (except the last) with tension that pulls into the next — a named unanswered pressure, not a bureaucratic “next we discuss.”

## What to cut

- Restating the previous sentence in new words  
- Empty intensifiers (very, extremely, really)  
- Hedging when the pack’s evidence is solid  
- Throat-clearing (“It is worth noting that…”)  
- Telling the reader how to feel instead of showing facts that produce the feeling  
- Guiding-question blockquotes and “check yourself” lists (those belong in the **lesson** pack, not here)  
- Process/meta language (ledger, skill names, “as the research showed”)

## Density

Research (the lesson pack) is broad. The narrative is **narrow and dense**. Every paragraph must advance the Core Message, deepen understanding, or land an emotional beat. Prefer one strong example over three medium ones.

## Mental-model close

End the whole piece with a clear shift in how the past looks — not a business “call to action,” not a syllabus recap. The reader should leave able to retell the arc in their own words.
